# Locally Perfect, Collectively Destructive

**Status:** Draft v3
**Word count:** ~550

---

Every decision made sense at the time. That's what made it so hard to see.

I was running treasury analytics at a regional bank. We produced two related forecasts—NII (how the balance sheet earns over time) and EVE (how it would be valued today). These use similar but distinct assumptions: rate scenarios are immediate shocks in EVE but gradual ramps in NII, some drivers make sense held fixed in one context but dynamic in another.

We kept getting flagged by risk partners for inconsistencies. Every meeting felt defensive—they'd point to a discrepancy, we'd explain why it existed, everyone would nod skeptically. Our documentation said "Is GDP used in both forecasts? Yes." That answered whether the same drivers appeared, but nothing about whether they were treated consistently.

I had Sylvia build a different table. Every model got a row. Columns captured not just which drivers we used, but the *source* and *forecasting approach* for each metric.

We stared at that chart and were flabbergasted.

Same metric—GDP, consumer confidence, 3-month Treasury—different *sources* between frameworks. GDP came from the Fed for EVE but from QRM for NII. Same metric, different *forecasting treatments* between models within the same framework: GDP held at last observed value for checking acquisitions, allowed to vary for mortgage paydowns. On top of blatant inconsistencies between NII and EVE for identical models.

Each choice was locally rational. Someone building the checking model had a good reason to hold GDP fixed. Someone building mortgage paydowns had a good reason to let it vary. Both right in isolation. But we were forecasting the balance sheet as a system. The collective inconsistency was incoherent.

The fix took two pages: "from" and "to" for standardizing variable treatments. A 60-minute meeting wrapped in 8 minutes. Our risk partners saw that we'd actually evaluated the system holistically and gave us the thumbs up. That credibility compounded for years.

The phrase I keep coming back to: *locally perfect, collectively destructive*.

This happens constantly in analytics work. Someone builds a component that's elegant and justified. Someone else builds an adjacent component, equally justified. Nobody owns the system-level view. Years pass. The inconsistencies become load-bearing—everyone routes around them, treats them as immutable.

I got lucky here. It's rare for a process this important to have a fix this obvious. Most of the time you step back, build the table, and find that the inconsistencies are genuinely hard—real tradeoffs with no clean answer. You don't get the 60-minute meeting that wraps in 8.

But the discipline still matters. If you're building systems from scratch, think about system-level consistency from the start. Document not just what drivers you're using but where they come from and how they're treated. Make the complexity visible before it calcifies.

And if you've inherited a system—which is most of us, most of the time—at least know that the exercise is worth doing. You probably won't find an easy win. But you might. And either way, you'll understand what you're actually working with.

Sylvia, by the way, became one of the strongest systems thinkers on the team. That exercise taught her something hard to learn abstractly: the component isn't the unit of analysis. The system is.
