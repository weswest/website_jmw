# Locally Perfect, Collectively Destructive

**Status:** Draft v2
**Word count:** ~320

---

Every decision made sense at the time. That's what made it so hard to see.

I was running treasury analytics at a regional bank. We produced two related forecasts—NII and EVE—that used similar inputs but kept getting flagged by risk partners for inconsistencies. Every meeting felt defensive: they'd point to a discrepancy, we'd explain why it existed, everyone would nod skeptically.

I had Sylvia build a table. Every model got a row. Columns captured not just which drivers we used, but the *source* and *forecasting approach* for each.

We stared at that chart and were flabbergasted.

Same metric, different sources between frameworks. Same metric, different forecasting treatments between models within the same framework. GDP held fixed here, allowed to vary there. Each choice locally rational—defensible in its narrow context. But collectively incoherent.

The fix took two pages: "from" and "to" for standardizing variable treatments. A 60-minute meeting wrapped in 8 minutes. Our risk partners looked at it, saw that we'd actually evaluated the system holistically, and gave us the thumbs up.

That's when I landed on the phrase: *locally perfect, collectively destructive*.

Someone optimizes their component. Someone else optimizes an adjacent component. Both are right in isolation. Nobody owns the system-level view. Years pass. The inconsistencies become load-bearing.

The unlock is usually embarrassingly simple. Build a table. Make the complexity visible. Document what's actually happening across the full system. The act of writing it down reveals what years of operating couldn't.

Most people never step back far enough to see it. The ones who do become invaluable.
