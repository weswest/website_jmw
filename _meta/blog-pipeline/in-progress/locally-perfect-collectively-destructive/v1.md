# Locally Perfect, Collectively Destructive

**Status:** Draft v1
**Word count:** ~750
**Series:** Understanding the Sausage (Article 2)

---

Treasury analytics produces two related but distinct forecasts: NII (net interest income—how the balance sheet earns over time) and EVE (economic value of equity—how the balance sheet would be valued today). For operational reasons, these use similar but distinct assumptions. Rate scenarios are immediate shocks in EVE but gradual 12-month ramps in NII. Some non-rate measures make more sense held fixed in one context but dynamic in another.

We kept getting bogged down explaining inconsistencies to our risk partners. Every meeting felt defensive. They'd point to a discrepancy, we'd explain why it existed, they'd nod skeptically, and we'd move on without anyone feeling great about it.

I had a vague notion of how the pieces fit together, but I hadn't dug deep. Our documentation essentially said: "Is GDP used in both the NII and EVE forecast? Yes. Move on." That answered whether the same drivers appeared in both—but nothing about whether they were treated consistently.

I had Sylvia build a different table. Every model for every aspect of the balance sheet got its own row. Columns captured the NII drivers and EVE drivers, but critically: she wrote in the *source* and *forecasting approach* for each metric.

We stared at that chart and were flabbergasted.

The same metric—GDP, consumer confidence, 3-month Treasury—would have different *sources* between EVE and NII. GDP came from the Fed for EVE but from QRM for NII. The same metric's *forecast* was inconsistent between models within the same framework: GDP was held at last observed value for checking acquisition forecasts but allowed to vary for mortgage paydown forecasts. On top of blatant inconsistencies between NII and EVE calculations for identical models.

Every one of those decisions made sense at the time. Each was locally rational—someone had thought about that specific model, that specific input, and made a defensible choice in that narrow context. But we were forecasting the balance sheet as a system. Stepping back made it clear: the collective inconsistency was intolerable.

There was no way to perfectly align NII and EVE. The underlying calculations were distinct enough that some discrepancy was required. But we could standardize variable treatments, align sources where possible, and document the remaining differences with clear rationale.

Sylvia and I put together two pages: "from" and "to" for how we'd standardize. Our risk partners took one look, gave us the thumbs up, and that 60-minute meeting wrapped in 8 minutes.

More importantly, it reset the relationship. They knew at that point that we were evaluating things holistically—that we'd step back far enough to see the system, not just defend individual components. That credibility compounded for years.

The phrase I keep coming back to: *locally perfect, collectively destructive*.

Each modeling decision had been optimized for its own context. The person building the checking acquisition model had a good reason to hold GDP fixed. The person building the mortgage paydown model had a good reason to let it vary. Both were right in isolation. But nobody had stepped back to ask whether the collective approach made sense—whether a risk partner looking at the full picture would find it coherent.

This happens constantly in analytics work. Someone builds a component that's elegant and well-justified. Someone else builds an adjacent component, equally elegant, equally justified. The components don't talk to each other. Nobody owns the system-level view. Years pass. The accumulated inconsistencies become load-bearing—everyone routes around them, explains them away, treats them as immutable facts of life.

The unlock is usually embarrassingly simple. Build a table. Make the complexity visible. Force yourself to write down what's actually happening across the full system. The act of documenting often reveals what years of operating couldn't.

Sylvia, by the way, went on to become one of the strongest systems thinkers on the team. That exercise—building the matrix, seeing the inconsistencies, working through standardization—taught her something that's hard to learn abstractly: the component isn't the unit of analysis. The system is.

That's the skill I try to develop in my teams now. Not just "do your piece well," but "understand how your piece interacts with everything else, and flag when the collective doesn't make sense."

Most people never step back far enough to see it. The ones who do become invaluable.
