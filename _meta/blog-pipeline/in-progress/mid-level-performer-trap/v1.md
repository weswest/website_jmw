# The Mid-Level Performer Trap

**Series:** Leading Through Analytics
**Source:** Q2 Interview Transcript
**Status:** Draft v1
**Target length:** ~1,800 words

---

The most dangerous employees aren't the ones who are obviously struggling. Those people get managed out, or they improve, or they self-select into different roles. The system handles them.

The dangerous ones are the employees who are fine. Not great, not terrible. They hit their deadlines. They don't cause drama. They've been doing the job for years and nobody has any specific complaints. They're just... fine.

I didn't understand this until I inherited a treasury analytics team at a large regional bank and watched one "fine" performer's departure reveal years of accumulated dysfunction that nobody had thought to question.

## The Setup

I'd been brought in to run asset liability strategy. The team I inherited was functional but demoralized—employee satisfaction scores were deeply negative, and the work had devolved into pure regulatory compliance. Check the boxes, keep the CFO from yelling, go home.

One of my direct reports was a woman I'll call Sarah. She'd been with the bank for several years and was responsible for quarterly model accuracy monitoring. This is important work—you're validating that the models predicting balance sheet behavior are actually performing as expected. If they're not, you need to know before it shows up in an earnings surprise.

Sarah was fine at this job. She met her deadlines. The reports got produced. When I asked how things were going, she'd say fine. When I asked if she needed anything, she'd say no. She clocked in around 8:30, left around 5, and if something went wrong, her attitude was basically "whatever, not my problem."

Here's the thing: I couldn't really complain about her. I had no specific performance issue to point to. The work was getting done. It just... took a while. The quarterly monitoring cycle consumed about ten weeks of her time each quarter. That seemed like a lot, but I'd never done this specific job at this specific bank, so what did I know?

This was actually my first time managing a team where I didn't know how to do the work myself. Every previous job, I'd come up through the ranks doing exactly what my team did. In consulting, you start as the analyst taking notes, then you run data requests, then you lead client meetings, all the way up. Even managing large teams, I could always roll up my sleeves and do any individual job if needed.

But model validation in a bank's treasury function? I understood the concepts, but I didn't have my hands dirty in the specific regulatory requirements, the specific data pipelines, the specific institutional knowledge. I had to trust that Sarah knew what she was doing.

So I did. For a while.

## The Reveal

Sarah eventually left for another opportunity. Fine, we were a little sad to see her go, whatever. We moved her responsibilities to another analyst on the team—I'll call her Lin.

Lin looked at Sarah's process and thought it was insane.

I don't mean she found a few inefficiencies. I mean she looked at the whole thing—the manual data pulls, the Excel gymnastics, the reconciliation steps, the review cycles—and concluded that the entire approach was fundamentally wrong. Not wrong in a "this violates best practices" way. Wrong in a "why would anyone do it this way" way.

Lin spent about two months re-architecting the entire process. She automated the data pipelines. She built validation checks that caught errors upstream instead of requiring manual review downstream. She eliminated entire categories of work that existed only because previous steps were unreliable.

The result: work that used to take ten weeks was now getting done in a week and a half.

Let that sink in. Same deliverable. Same regulatory requirements. Same data. Ten weeks became one and a half weeks. That's not a 20% improvement. That's an 85% reduction in effort.

## The Insight

Here's what made this so unsettling: Lin didn't care about the job any more than Sarah did. She wasn't some passionate high-performer who bled company colors. She showed up, did her work, went home. In terms of raw motivation and engagement, she and Sarah were basically identical.

The difference was that Lin was lazy in that specific programmer way—she hated doing repetitive manual work so much that she'd invest significant effort upfront to avoid it later. Sarah would do the ten-week slog every quarter, quarter after quarter, because that's what the job was. Lin looked at the ten-week slog and thought, "absolutely not, there has to be a better way."

This completely changed how I think about performance evaluation.

We tend to assess people on two dimensions: how much they care (motivation/attitude) and how capable they are (skills/intelligence). A motivated, capable person is an A player. An unmotivated, less capable person is managed out. Simple enough.

But there's a third dimension that's mostly invisible in traditional performance management: does this person instinctively look for ways to make the future easier?

Some people will do the work in front of them. They'll do it fine. They'll do it for years. But they'll never ask whether the work should exist in the first place, whether there's a fundamentally better approach, whether the process they inherited makes any sense. They execute tasks. They don't question systems.

Other people can't help but question systems. Not because they're more engaged or more motivated—sometimes the opposite. They question systems because tolerating unnecessary friction is physically painful to them. They'd rather spend a month fixing a broken process than spend another quarter grinding through it.

The terrifying thing is that from a traditional management perspective, these two types of people look identical until something forces the comparison.

## The Trap

This is the mid-level performer trap: people can be "fine" for years while quietly accumulating massive inefficiency that nobody thinks to question.

Sarah wasn't sandbagging. She wasn't being lazy in the pejorative sense. She genuinely thought ten weeks was what this work required, because that's what it had always required, because that's how she'd always done it, because that's what she'd been trained to do when she started. She was executing the process she'd inherited.

And because the work got done, and because she didn't cause problems, and because I didn't know enough about the specific work to question it, and because "ten weeks" became the baseline expectation that everyone just accepted—the inefficiency compounded invisibly for years.

How many Sarahs are in your organization right now? How much work is taking ten weeks that could take one and a half? How would you even know?

That's the trap. The Sarahs don't show up in your performance data. They don't miss deadlines. They don't have conflict with colleagues. They pass their annual reviews because there's nothing specific to criticize. They're fine.

Meanwhile, the Lins often look interchangeable with the Sarahs on paper. They might even seem less engaged—they're the ones pushing back on requests, asking annoying questions about why things are done a certain way, refusing to just do it the way it's always been done. In a culture that values compliance and consistency, the Lin behavior pattern can actually read as problematic.

## What I Do Differently Now

I've become deeply suspicious of "fine."

When someone's been doing a job the same way for years with no issues, I no longer assume that means the job is being done well. It might mean that. Or it might mean that nobody's bothered to ask whether the job makes sense.

I specifically look for the Lin pattern when hiring and promoting: people who've demonstrated an instinct for eliminating unnecessary work, not just executing it efficiently. Past behavior where someone said "this is stupid, let me fix it" is more predictive than almost any other signal.

I try to create conditions where questioning existing processes is rewarded rather than punished. This is harder than it sounds. Most organizations have immune systems that resist this kind of questioning. "That's how we've always done it" is a powerful force.

And when someone leaves, I treat it as an opportunity for a process audit, not just a backfill. Don't just move the work to someone else. Ask whether the work should exist in its current form. You might be about to discover that you've been lighting ten weeks on fire every quarter for years.

The uncomfortable truth is that if you haven't found a Sarah situation in your organization, you probably just haven't looked hard enough yet.
