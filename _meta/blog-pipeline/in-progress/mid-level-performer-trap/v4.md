# When Someone Can't Explain Their Job, Pay Attention

**Status:** Draft v4 (per writing guide)
**Word count:** ~650

---

If someone can't explain their work simply, either they don't understand it or the work doesn't make sense. Both are problems worth investigating.

I learned this running a treasury analytics team at a regional bank. One of my reports—call her Sarah—handled quarterly model validation. Ten weeks of work each quarter. When I asked her to walk me through the process, she couldn't. The explanation was tangled, circular, full of "well, first we have to..." without ever landing on why.

I had twenty other problems. Culture was broken. Leadership needed replacing. I was trying to shift the team from regulatory box-checking to actual strategic value. Sarah's tangled explanation wasn't screaming for attention—the work got done, deadlines got met—so I filed it under "revisit later" and moved on.

That was a mistake.

Sarah eventually quit. We moved her work to another analyst, Jianglan. Jianglan looked at the process and concluded the entire approach was wrong—not inefficient, *wrong*. She spent two months rebuilding it. Ten weeks became a week and a half. Same deliverable, same requirements. 85% reduction.

Here's what I got wrong: Sarah's inability to explain the work *was* the signal. When someone's been doing a job for years and can't articulate what they're actually doing or why, that's not a communication problem. That's a process that's calcified beyond anyone's understanding, including the person doing it.

I should have pushed through the tangled explanation. Should have sat with her and untangled it together, because untangling would have revealed the dysfunction. But I didn't have the patience, and I told myself I didn't have the time. The twenty urgent problems crowded out the one quiet problem that turned out to be an 85% efficiency gain hiding in plain sight.

The deeper issue: Sarah wasn't a bad employee. She was fine. Met deadlines, no drama, years of experience. "Fine" is what made this invisible. The struggling employees get managed out. The system handles them. The fine employees accumulate dysfunction for years because nobody has a specific complaint.

Andy Grove said he regretted 100% of his personnel decisions where he waited to see if things would get better. I've found this painfully accurate. But the Sarah situation is harder than that—I wasn't waiting on a problem I'd identified. I'd identified a signal and dismissed it because I couldn't immediately see what it meant.

Jianglan, by the way, wasn't more motivated than Sarah. Same clock-in, clock-out energy. The difference: she was allergic to unnecessary work. She'd invest a month rebuilding something to avoid doing it the hard way forever. Sarah would do the ten-week slog quarterly until retirement because that's what the job was.

We evaluate people on capability and motivation. But there's a third axis: does this person instinctively question whether the work should exist in its current form? That's the axis that's invisible until you get a comparison.

I've gotten better at this. When someone can't explain their process clearly, I treat it as a flag now, not a frustration. I make time to untangle it with them, because the untangling is where the insight lives. It's slower than I'd like. It requires patience I had to develop. But the alternative is walking past 85% efficiency gains because they're hidden inside tangled explanations from employees who are doing just fine.
