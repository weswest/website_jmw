# The Mid-Level Performer Trap

**Status:** Draft v3
**Target length:** ~600 words

---

The most dangerous employees aren't the ones who are struggling. Those people get managed out or improve. The system handles them.

The dangerous ones are the employees who are fine. They hit deadlines. They don't cause drama. Nobody has specific complaints. They're just... fine.

I learned this the hard way when I took over a treasury analytics team at a large regional bank. One of my reports—I'll call her Sarah—was responsible for quarterly model accuracy monitoring. She'd been doing it for years. The work took about ten weeks each quarter. That seemed like a lot, but I'd never done this specific job at this specific bank, and this was my first time managing work I didn't know how to do myself.

Something felt off. My gut said ten weeks didn't make sense. But I was juggling twenty other problems, and when I asked Sarah to walk me through the process, she couldn't explain it clearly. The description was tangled, and I didn't have the patience to help her untangle it. That's on me—I've since learned that when someone can't explain their work simply, that's a signal worth investigating, not dismissing.

Then Sarah quit.

We moved her work to another analyst, Lin. Lin looked at Sarah's process and thought it was insane. Not "some inefficiencies"—the entire approach was wrong. Lin spent two months rebuilding it.

The result: ten weeks became a week and a half. Same deliverable. Same requirements. 85% reduction.

Here's the honest version: I should have caught this, and I didn't.

Andy Grove said he regretted 100% of his personnel decisions where he chose to "wait and see if things get better." I've found this painfully accurate. But I understand why I waited. Sarah wasn't failing. She was fine. And confronting a "fine" performer feels cruel when you can't point to a specific failure. You're questioning their value, their years of experience. So you put it off.

What made this harder to see: Lin didn't care about the job any more than Sarah did. Same motivation, same engagement. The difference was Lin was lazy in that programmer way—she'd invest a month of effort to avoid repetitive work. Sarah would do the ten-week slog forever because that's what the job was.

We evaluate people on how much they care and how capable they are. But there's a third dimension: does this person instinctively look for ways to make the future easier? Some people execute tasks. Others can't help but question whether the tasks should exist. These two types look identical until something forces the comparison.

The people stuff pays bigger dividends than almost any other change you can make. But it's deeply uncomfortable—you're messing with someone's livelihood. So we avoid it. We tell ourselves we'll get to the quiet problems later.

I've since learned that turnaround situations require about 50% more effort than you expect, just to understand what's broken. The dysfunction is load-bearing. You can't see it until you try to remove it. Sarah's ten-week process looked fine because "fine" had become the baseline everyone accepted.

I don't have a clean framework for this. What I know is that "fine" makes me nervous now. When someone's been doing a job the same way for years with no issues, I no longer assume the job is being done well. It might mean nobody's questioned it.

And when someone leaves—even someone fine—I treat it as a process audit, not just a backfill.

Sarah probably wasn't the only ten-week-job-that-should-have-been-one-week in that organization. She was just the one I found out about.
