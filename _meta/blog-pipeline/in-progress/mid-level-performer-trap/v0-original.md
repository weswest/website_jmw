# From Box-Checking to Strategic Powerhouse: How We Transformed a Treasury Analytics Team

**Series:** Leading Through Analytics
**Source:** Q2 Interview Transcript
**Status:** Draft v1
**Target length:** ~1,500 words
**LinkedIn ready:** No

---

When I took over the asset liability strategy team at a large regional bank, employee satisfaction scores were at -45. When I left, they were at +92. We'd also tripled headcount.

People often ask what I did. The honest answer is that most of the transformation came from one simple shift: we stopped being a team that checked regulatory boxes and started being a team that shaped strategy.

## The Problem: Death by Zero-Error Mandate

The team I inherited was paralyzed by fear of mistakes.

The previous leader—let's call him David—had been burned a few times when numbers went out incorrectly. Leadership had come down hard on him. His response was understandable but catastrophic: he instituted a zero-error policy that meant every output got reviewed collectively, line by line.

We called it "the vortex." David would pull his entire team of five analysts into his office for three-hour sessions, going cell by cell through spreadsheets. They'd spend 15 hours collectively to find one rate pointer coded incorrectly for one-fifteenth of the balance sheet.

It was a massive waste of time. Worse, it was demoralizing. People weren't developing skills or judgment—they were just staring at numbers together, hoping someone would spot a typo.

## The Mindset Shift: From Compliance to Strategy

The first thing I did was reframe what we were actually doing.

Asset liability management, done well, is the brain of the bank. We know how the entire balance sheet will evolve over time. That's not a regulatory burden—that's an incredibly rich data source for strategic decision-making. When someone on the lending side wants to know "if I do X, what happens to our colleagues on the deposit side?"—we're the ones who can answer that.

Once people understood that their work shaped earnings calls and strategic decisions, everything changed. They weren't checking boxes so the CFO wouldn't yell at them. They were providing intelligence that mattered.

I removed David from the leadership equation and promoted one of his senior analysts—a guy who got it—into that role. Not because David was a bad person, but because his trauma had created a culture that couldn't scale.

## The Mid-Level Performer Trap

This was my first time managing a team where I didn't know how to do the work myself.

Every previous job, I'd been first among equals. In consulting, you come up through the ranks—you've been the first-year taking notes, the second-year running data requests, the third-year leading client meetings, all the way up. Even managing big teams, I'd done every job at some point.

But here I was managing quantitative model monitoring and validation work I'd never done at this specific bank, with this specific regulatory framework. I had to trust people.

That's when I learned a hard lesson about mid-level performers.

There was a woman on the team—I'll call her Sarah—responsible for quarterly model accuracy monitoring. She seemed fine. You couldn't really complain about her performance. She clocked in at 8:30, clocked out at 4:57, and if things didn't go well, her attitude was "whatever, not my problem."

When Sarah left, we moved her work to another analyst—let's call her Lin. Lin looked at Sarah's process and thought it was insane. Over two months, she re-architected everything.

**The result: work that used to take 10 weeks was now getting done in a week and a half.**

This fundamentally changed how I think about performance. It's not just about caring versus not caring, or smart versus not smart. There's a third axis: *does this person instinctively look for ways to make the future easier, or do they just do the work in front of them?*

Lin didn't care about the job in some passionate sense. But she was "lazy" in that programmer way—she hated inefficiency so much that she'd invest heavily today to avoid repetitive work tomorrow. Sarah would never even think about that tradeoff.

Since then, I've become deeply skeptical of comfortable mid-level performers. Not because they're bad people, but because "fine" performance can mask years of accumulated inefficiency that nobody's questioning.

## Managing Work You Don't Understand

When you can't do the work yourself, you have to develop different instincts.

I couldn't check Lin's model validation methodology line by line. But I could:

- **Watch for velocity changes.** When Lin turned 10 weeks into 1.5 weeks, that told me something important—either about her, or about what Sarah had been doing.
- **Ask "why this way?"** Not to catch mistakes, but to understand reasoning. People who've thought deeply about their approach can explain it. People running on autopilot can't.
- **Look for systematic improvement.** Good people don't just complete tasks—they leave the process better than they found it.

The trap is thinking you need to understand the technical details to manage technical people. You don't. You need to understand whether they're thinking critically about their own work.

## Building an Empire (Not Really)

One of my senior analysts used to joke that he was watching me "build an empire."

I'd push back on that framing every time. What I told him—and what I genuinely believe—is that the only way I stay excited about a job is to find something badly broken, figure out how to fix it, get it into maintenance mode, and then take on new challenges.

That's not empire building. That's just... how I stay engaged.

But I think the team picked up on something real: the poor perception from senior leadership, the underinvestment in resources—none of that was inevitable. We could change it. And watching that change happen, being part of it, made people satisfied in a way that "doing your job adequately" never does.

## What I Learned

**Purpose beats process.** The vortex was a process solution to what was actually a purpose problem. When people understand *why* their work matters, they find better ways to make it accurate without soul-crushing reviews.

**Comfortable performance hides rot.** Someone doing "fine" for years might be masking massive inefficiency. It's worth periodically asking whether work could be done fundamentally differently.

**You don't need to do the work to manage the work.** You need to know what good thinking looks like, and create conditions where people can do their best thinking.

**Culture change is fast when it's real.** We went from -45 to +92 satisfaction not through team-building exercises or pizza parties, but by genuinely changing what the team was for. When the change is substantive, people feel it immediately.
