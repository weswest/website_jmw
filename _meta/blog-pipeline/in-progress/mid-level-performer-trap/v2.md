# The Mid-Level Performer Trap

**Series:** Leading Through Analytics
**Source:** Q2 Interview Transcript + additional context
**Status:** Draft v2
**Target length:** ~2,000 words

---

The most dangerous employees aren't the ones who are obviously struggling. Those people get managed out, or they improve, or they self-select into different roles. The system handles them.

The dangerous ones are the employees who are fine. Not great, not terrible. They hit their deadlines. They don't cause drama. They've been doing the job for years and nobody has any specific complaints. They're just... fine.

I didn't understand this until I inherited a treasury analytics team at a large regional bank. And I'm not going to pretend I figured it out through some brilliant management insight. I got lucky. Someone quit, and that forced a revelation I should have found on my own.

## Twenty Fires Burning

When I took over the team, I was fighting about twenty fires simultaneously.

Employee satisfaction scores were deeply negative. The team's reputation with senior leadership was garbage. We were understaffed, under-resourced, and the work had devolved into pure regulatory compliance—check the boxes, keep the CFO from yelling, go home. The team lead had been burned by past mistakes and had instituted a culture of collective paranoia, pulling five people into his office for hours at a time to review numbers line by line.

So I was busy. Really busy. Rebuilding culture. Replacing leaders. Fighting for budget. Trying to shift the team's identity from "regulatory box-checkers" to "strategic asset." Each of those twenty fires felt urgent. Each one demanded attention.

And then there was fire number twenty-one, which I didn't even know was burning.

## Sarah

One of my direct reports was a woman I'll call Sarah. She'd been with the bank for several years and was responsible for quarterly model accuracy monitoring—validating that the models predicting balance sheet behavior were actually performing as expected.

Sarah was fine at this job. She met her deadlines. The reports got produced. When I asked how things were going, she'd say fine. When I asked if she needed anything, she'd say no. She clocked in around 8:30, left around 5, and if something went wrong, her attitude was basically "whatever, not my problem."

I couldn't really complain about her. The work was getting done. It just took a while—about ten weeks each quarter. That seemed like a lot, but I'd never done this specific job at this specific bank.

This was actually the first time in my career I was managing a team where I didn't know how to do the work myself. Every previous job, I'd come up through the ranks. In consulting, you start as the analyst taking notes, then run data requests, then lead client meetings. You've done every job on the way up. Here, I had to trust that Sarah knew what she was doing.

Something felt off. My spidey sense was telling me the ten-week cycle didn't make sense. But I had nineteen other fires actively burning, and this one wasn't screaming for attention. Sarah wasn't complaining. The work was getting done. I moved on to the next crisis.

Then Sarah quit.

## What the Departure Revealed

We moved Sarah's responsibilities to another analyst—I'll call her Lin. Lin looked at Sarah's process and thought it was insane.

Not "a few inefficiencies." The entire approach was fundamentally wrong. The manual data pulls, the Excel gymnastics, the reconciliation steps—Lin concluded that the work had been designed by someone who didn't understand what they were actually trying to accomplish, and then calcified into "the process" through years of repetition.

Lin spent two months re-architecting everything. Automated the pipelines. Built validation checks upstream. Eliminated entire categories of work that only existed because previous steps were unreliable.

The result: ten weeks became a week and a half. Same deliverable, same regulatory requirements, same data. An 85% reduction.

## What I Got Wrong

Here's the honest version of this story: I should have caught this, and I didn't.

Andy Grove once said he regretted 100% of his personnel decisions where he chose to "wait and see if things get better." I've found this to be painfully accurate. The wait-and-see approach on people almost never works. Things don't get better on their own. The discomfort you're avoiding by not acting just compounds into bigger discomfort later.

But I also understand why I didn't act. Sarah wasn't failing. She was fine. And the people stuff is the most insidious challenge in any organization. You're messing with someone's livelihood, their identity, their sense of self-worth. Confronting a "fine" performer about whether they're actually fine feels cruel, especially when you can't point to a specific failure.

So instead I waited. Not consciously—I didn't make a deliberate decision to wait. I just had twenty other fires burning, and this one didn't seem that urgent. Sarah quit, which forced the issue, but I should have pushed harder when my gut was telling me something was wrong.

## The Third Dimension

What made this so hard to see is that Lin didn't care about the job any more than Sarah did. She wasn't some passionate high-performer bleeding company colors. In terms of motivation and engagement, she and Sarah were basically identical.

The difference was that Lin was lazy in that specific programmer way—she hated doing repetitive manual work so much that she'd invest heavily upfront to avoid it later. Sarah would do the ten-week slog every quarter, forever, because that's what the job was. Lin looked at the ten-week slog and thought: absolutely not.

We tend to evaluate people on two dimensions: how much they care and how capable they are. But there's a third dimension that's mostly invisible: does this person instinctively look for ways to make the future easier?

Some people execute tasks. They do them fine. They do them for years. But they never ask whether the tasks should exist, whether there's a better approach, whether the process they inherited makes any sense.

Other people can't help but question systems. Not because they're more engaged—sometimes less. They question systems because tolerating unnecessary friction is physically painful to them.

From a traditional management perspective, these two types look identical until something forces the comparison.

## The Turnaround Tax

I've since moved on to other roles, and I've learned something about turnaround situations specifically: you have to put in about 50% more effort than you think necessary, just to understand what's actually broken.

When you walk into a functioning organization, the problems are legible. Metrics are tracked. Issues are surfaced. There's institutional knowledge about what works and what doesn't.

When you walk into a turnaround, you don't even know which fires are burning. Some of them look fine on the surface—like Sarah's ten-week process—because "fine" has become the baseline everyone accepts. The dysfunction is load-bearing. You can't see it until you try to remove it.

I thought I was paying attention to the twenty fires I could see. I wasn't paying attention to the twenty-first fire—the one that looked fine because nobody had questioned it in years.

## Why This Is So Hard

A CEO I know jokes that his job is managing the list of "things that MUST BE RESOLVED YESTERDAY" and deciding which one or two he's actually going to focus on today. That's the reality of leadership: there's always more broken than you can fix.

The people stuff pays bigger dividends faster than almost any other change you can make. Getting the right people in the right roles, fixing broken processes, building capability—this is the lifeblood of an organization. It compounds in ways that technology investments and strategic pivots usually don't.

But it's also deeply uncomfortable. When you confront a "fine" performer, you're not just questioning their work. You're questioning their value, their judgment, maybe their years of experience. When you let someone go, you're disrupting their life, their family, their mortgage. The human cost is real and immediate. The organizational benefit is diffuse and delayed.

So we avoid it. We put out the fires that are screaming loudest. We tell ourselves we'll get to the quiet problems later. And the quiet problems compound for years.

## What I Think Now

I don't have a clean framework for this. Life doesn't work that way.

What I know is that "fine" should make me nervous now, where it didn't before. When someone's been doing a job the same way for years with no issues, I no longer assume that means the job is being done well. It might mean nobody's bothered to ask whether the job makes sense.

I try to listen to my gut when something feels off, even when I can't articulate why, even when I don't have time to investigate. The spidey sense is usually right. The ten-week process that seems too long probably is too long.

And when someone leaves—even someone "fine"—I try to treat it as a process audit, not just a backfill. The departure might reveal years of accumulated dysfunction that everyone had just accepted as normal.

But mostly I've accepted that I'm going to miss things. There's always a twenty-first fire you don't know about. The best you can do is stay suspicious of comfort, stay curious about "the way things have always been done," and act faster when your instincts are telling you something's wrong.

The uncomfortable truth is that Sarah probably wasn't the only ten-week-job-that-should-have-been-one-week in that organization. She was just the one I found out about.
