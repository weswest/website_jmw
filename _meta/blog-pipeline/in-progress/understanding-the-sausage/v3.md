# Understanding the Sausage

**Status:** Draft v3
**Word count:** ~700

---

I've run predictive analytics units, both as a practitioner at large banks and as a consultant supporting even larger ones. There's always tension in C-level steercos: how deep in the guts of the modeling should we get?

The answer is almost always to lead with the key takeaways, load the hell out of the appendix with gory details, and see where they want to take the conversation. The nature of business is that senior people pay others to handle operational details. They're paid to make judgment calls, and at a certain point they have to trust that their team has validated the details.

I'm bad at a lot of things, but I've made a career being good at landing at the right level of complexity for senior stakeholders. The path to get there, though, is to understand the entire chain of work so deeply that I know which pieces are important, which pieces are less critical, and how the system changes as a whole. I need to understand how changes to scenarios, customer behaviors, or other inputs will ripple through to outcomes. I become a little mini-forecasting engine where leaders can throw out ideas and I can confidently share how our forecasts would evolve.

Personally, I have to understand the complexity before I can explain something simply.

That's bitten me—but not in the way you'd expect.

I was running a treasury analytics team at a regional bank. One of my skip-level reports handled quarterly model validation. Ten weeks of work each cycle. When her manager asked her to explain the process, she couldn't do it clearly. Circular explanations, dependencies that didn't obviously need to exist. Her manager flagged it to me, but neither of us pushed hard enough to untangle it.

She eventually left. Her replacement rebuilt the whole thing. Ten weeks became a week and a half.

Here's what I missed: the problem wasn't that I needed to personally interrogate this complexity. I had twenty other problems and couldn't dive into every process. The problem was that I hadn't equipped her manager—my direct report—to do that interrogation herself.

The modeling manager knew something felt off. She'd surfaced it. But she didn't have the framework to push through the tangled explanation and ask the harder questions: Does this process make sense? Has anyone evaluated whether the effort is proportionate to the output? What would it take to rebuild this, and what's the payback period?

We worked through this one together after the fact—diagnosed what we'd missed, talked through how she could have caught it earlier. Then we applied the same lens to other processes on her team. She got better at recognizing when complexity was justified versus when it was accumulated dysfunction. She learned to ask about evaluation horizons: is the team optimizing within a broken process, or have they stepped back to ask whether the process should exist?

She's since gone on to have an excellent career as a respected problem solver. That skill—interrogating complexity with appropriate skepticism—became one of her defining strengths.

The lesson for me wasn't "dive deeper into the details yourself." I can't scale that. The lesson was that my job is to develop managers who can do this interrogation, who know which questions to ask, who aren't satisfied with "that's how we've always done it."

You can't personally understand every process in your organization. But you can build a team that's collectively allergic to unjustified complexity—that treats tangled explanations as a signal worth investigating, not a frustration to route around.

That's the multiplier. That's what scales.
