# Understanding the Sausage

**Status:** Draft v2
**Word count:** ~650

---

I've run predictive analytics units, both as a practitioner at large banks and as a consultant supporting even larger ones. There's always tension in C-level steercos: how deep in the guts of the modeling should we get?

The answer is almost always to lead with the key takeaways, load the hell out of the appendix with gory details, and see where they want to take the conversation. The nature of business is that senior people pay others to handle operational details. They're paid to make judgment calls, and at a certain point they have to trust that their team has validated the details.

I'm bad at a lot of things, but I've made a career being good at landing at the right level of complexity for senior stakeholders. The path to get there, though, is to understand the entire chain of work so deeply that I know which pieces are important, which pieces are less critical, and how the system changes as a whole. I need to understand how changes to scenarios, customer behaviors, or other inputs will ripple through to outcomes. I become a little mini-forecasting engine where leaders can throw out ideas and I can confidently share how our forecasts would evolve.

Personally, I have to understand the complexity before I can explain something simply.

That's bitten me.

I was running a treasury analytics team at a regional bank. One of my analysts handled quarterly model validation—ten weeks of work each cycle. When I asked her to explain the process, she couldn't do it clearly. Circular explanations, dependencies that didn't obviously need to exist.

I had twenty other problems. I didn't have patience to sit with her and untangle it. And critically: I didn't understand the work deeply enough myself to know which questions to ask. I'd inherited a process I couldn't explain simply, which meant I didn't actually understand it, which meant I couldn't evaluate whether it made sense.

She eventually left. Her replacement rebuilt the whole thing. Ten weeks became a week and a half.

The 85% reduction had been hiding in plain sight—inside complexity that nobody had honestly interrogated. Not her, because she'd inherited the process and assumed it was correct. Not me, because I hadn't invested the time to understand it deeply enough to see the dysfunction.

The evaluation horizon matters here. She was optimizing within the existing approach: doing the ten-week slog as efficiently as possible. The right move was stepping back and asking whether the ten-week slog should exist at all. That requires a two-month investment to save eight weeks per quarter indefinitely. Obvious math in retrospect. But nobody was doing the math because nobody understood the system well enough to question it.

This is the tension I think about now: you can't deeply understand every process in your organization. You shouldn't try. But you need enough fluency to recognize when complexity is justified versus when it's accumulated dysfunction that everyone's accepted as normal.

The signals I've learned to watch for: when someone can't explain their work simply, when effort seems wildly disproportionate to output, when "that's how we've always done it" is the justification. These don't mean something's wrong. But they mean it's worth investing the time to actually understand the sausage—because you can't simplify what you don't understand, and you can't improve what you can't see.
