# When to Understand How the Sausage Gets Made

**Status:** Draft v1
**Word count:** ~600

---

Senior leaders need answers, not process. Nobody in the C-suite wants a forty-minute walkthrough of your data pipeline. They want to know: will we hit the number? What's the risk? What do you need from me?

This is correct most of the time. Executives who get lost in operational details aren't doing their job. The whole point of building teams is that you trust people to handle the how while you focus on the what and why.

But there's a failure mode here that I've hit more than once: treating all complexity as someone else's problem.

At some point, understanding the details becomes a requirement—not to micromanage, but to answer three questions:

**Is the work being done correctly?** Not "are deadlines being met," but is the underlying approach sound? A team can hit every deadline while building on a foundation that's fundamentally broken.

**Is the work being done efficiently?** Not "is anyone complaining," but has anyone honestly interrogated whether the effort required makes sense? Processes calcify. What started as a reasonable approach becomes a ten-week slog that should take ten days, and nobody notices because "that's how long it takes."

**Is the work aligned with strategic objectives?** Not "does the output exist," but does the work actually serve what we're trying to accomplish? Teams can execute flawlessly on things that don't matter.

The person doing the work should be asking these questions. But often they're not—either because they inherited the process and assume it's correct, or because they're too close to see the dysfunction, or because they don't have visibility into strategic context, or because honestly interrogating their own work is uncomfortable.

So it falls to leadership to occasionally dive in. Not to check their work, but to pressure-test whether the complexity is justified.

I had an analyst doing quarterly model validation. Ten weeks each quarter. When I asked her to explain the process, she couldn't do it clearly—the explanation was circular, full of dependencies that didn't obviously need to exist. I had twenty other problems demanding attention, so I moved on. She eventually left, her replacement rebuilt the process from scratch, and ten weeks became a week and a half.

That's an 85% reduction I walked past because I treated the complexity as her domain, not mine.

The question I failed to ask: has anyone evaluated whether this process makes sense on an appropriate time horizon? She was optimizing within the existing approach—doing the ten-week slog as efficiently as possible. The right move was to step back and ask whether the ten-week slog should exist at all. That requires a two-month investment to potentially save eight weeks per quarter going forward. The math is obvious in retrospect. But nobody was doing the math because nobody had stepped back far enough to see it.

This is the macro-to-micro tension executives live in. You can't dive into every process. You shouldn't. But you need a sense for when complexity is load-bearing versus when it's accumulated dysfunction that everyone's accepted as normal.

The signals I watch for now: When someone can't explain their work simply. When effort seems disproportionate to output. When "that's how we've always done it" is the primary justification. When the person doing the work has never questioned whether the work should exist in its current form.

None of these mean something's wrong. But they mean it's worth understanding the sausage, just this once.
