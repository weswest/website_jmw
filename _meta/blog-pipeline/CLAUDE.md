C-suite hiring managers and potential advisory clients. NOT conference organizers (secondary), NOT developers, NOT recruiters.

### The positioning thesis
The implicit argument: **"This is someone who can go as deep as needed and come back with something you can act on."**

### Framing philosophy
- Frame everything as "no duh" statements about how good work gets done, not as personal accomplishments. The career arc and articles provide the proof.
- The writing is the strongest proof of the "deep AND communicates" thesis.
- Do great work and let the work speak for itself. Never claim superiority, competitive advantage, or how much better Wes is than anyone else. Ever.

## Four Pillars

**1. See all of the options.**
The first step isn't building — it's mapping the full solution space so you know what you're choosing between.

**2. Optimize the cost/benefit tradeoff.**
Accuracy, interpretability, speed to production, regulatory burden — every business decision is a multi-dimensional tradeoff. "Most accurate" or "highest ROI" is rarely the best answer.

**3. Execute efficiently.**
Decompose, sequence, find the bottleneck, solve it. The right sequencing means early deliverables generate value while the rest are still being built.

**4. Make it land.**
Analytics is only as valuable as the decision it changes.

## Voice, Style, and Writing Guide

### Tone and posture
- **"No duh" framing.** Statements should read as obvious truths about how good work gets done — not as personal accomplishments or marketing claims. The reader thinks "of course" and then realizes most people don't actually operate this way.
- **Professional, direct.** No buddy energy. No forced warmth. No "I'm passionate about..." language. The way a confident executive talks in a first meeting with a peer. Declarative, not performative.
- **Never self-aggrandizing.** The evidence makes it obvious. Let the reader draw the conclusion. Never talk about competitive advantage, being better than others, or how well Wes's brain works. The work speaks for itself.

### Language preferences
- **Verb nouns. Action-oriented language.** Favor constructions that do things over constructions that describe things.
- **Problem-framing over experience-framing.** Lead with the problem or the principle; let the experience appear naturally as support.
- **Outcome-first.** Don't describe what was done; describe what changed.
- **Cut ruthlessly.** Short + punchy beats thorough + accurate.
- **Avoid consulting jargon.** No "leveraging synergies," no "driving transformation," no "thought leader." Plain language a CEO would use in conversation.
- **Em dashes for asides, not parentheses.**

### Specific rhetorical devices
- **Paired/linked assertions.** When two ideas form a natural pair, use ellipsis linking ("Respect the decisions that got us here …" / "… Then change them.").
- **The complexity tax as signature metaphor.** Building something sophisticated you'll never deploy, specifically so you can have the right conversation about why the simpler thing is the right answer. Reference it, link to it.
- **Concrete specifics over abstract claims.** Name the rooms, name the roles, name the numbers.

### Broader themes across all writing
- **This is a time of change, not a victory lap.** Never cheer the displacement of workers or frame AI as "better than humans." It is objectively better to train humans to do these jobs. The point is that people need to learn to work with these tools — not that the tools replace people.
- **Humility over authority.** Frame insights as personal experience, not prescriptions. "None of this is canonical — I'm sharing what works for me."
- **Executing via an actor is a new skill.** The running theme: solo contributors have never needed to think about the "why" — they had a task, they controlled the domain. Working through agents requires articulating objectives, guardrails, and what good looks like. That demands humility and self-reflection most technical work has never required.
- **Self-knowledge, not superiority.** When comparing agent workflows to human workflows, frame the insight as what the process reveals about *your own* thinking — not why agents are better than people.

### LinkedIn post voice (distinct from article voice)
LinkedIn posts are more casual than articles. Same core philosophy — let the work speak for itself, never claim superiority, frame as personal experience — but the delivery is noticeably different:
- **Conversational, not editorial.** Write like you're telling a colleague about something that happened today, not writing for a publication.
- **Pop culture references welcome.** Rick and Morty Meseeks, etc. Signals "real person" and lands with the technical audience.
- **Self-deprecating humor about the tools.** "Let's see how it fails" energy. The system is impressive but imperfect, and that honesty is the hook.
- **No article links unless strategically withheld.** Keeping people in the LinkedIn feed drives engagement. Drop the link in a comment after traction, or leave it out entirely — the profile has the site URL.
- **Shorter, punchier, more fragmented.** Bold labels, one-line paragraphs, numbered progressions. LinkedIn readers skim aggressively.

### Publishing cadence
- **Publish day is Tuesday.** All new articles get a Tuesday date regardless of when they're actually written or pushed.

### Link conventions
- **Links to Wes's own writing are always absolute URLs to weswest.ai.** e.g., `https://weswest.ai/writing/ai-makes-everyone-a-manager/` — never relative paths, never links to the original publisher. This ensures links work regardless of where the article is published (site, LinkedIn, Substack).

### What NOT to do
- **Never mention the patent.** Not obliquely, not as "IP development," not as "novel proprietary architecture."
- **Never position as job-seeking.** No "open to opportunities," no "hire me" CTAs, no "available for" language.
- **Never claim superiority or competitive advantage.** No "this makes me better than," no "this is my edge," no framing of personal traits as advantages over others. This is Wes's entire philosophy for personal success: do great work and let it speak for itself. This rule is load-bearing — not just a stylistic preference.
- **Never describe Wes's brain, intelligence, or cognitive traits.** These are private. The work demonstrates capability; the writing never claims it.
- **Private context is not publishable content.** When Wes shares personal details to inform tone or direction (e.g., how his mind works, private frustrations), that is instruction for the AI, not content for the article. Never surface private framing in published copy.
- **Accuracy about actual mechanics matters.** Don't romanticize or simplify how the system actually works to make a paragraph read better. If the agent does the introspection (not Wes), say that. Get the mechanics right.
- **No blog-style casualness.** Everything is "Writing," treated as real content.
- **Don't over-explain.** Assertions don't need paragraphs of justification.

### Editorial mode: "choice points"
When presenting copy alternatives, show the variants first, then isolate the 2-4 specific decision axes with a one-line rationale for each option. This lets Wes make precise editorial decisions on the individual dimensions.

### How Wes edits
- He reacts to drafts. Give him something concrete to push back on. Verbose first drafts are fine — paring down is easier than adding.
- He'll tell you the *axes are wrong* before he'll wordsmith. Get the framing right first.
- Specific word choices matter — including small structural connectors ("— same" vs. "— which is the same"). He notices and corrects.
- He'll cut aggressively when something is too long.
- He gives raw, unpolished substance and expects it to be cleaned up while keeping the edge.
- Humor and self-deprecation about tools is welcome when honest and self-aware (e.g., "somewhat obsequious").
- He thinks in compounding systems. When you stop at the first-order observation (solo contributors need management skills), push to the systemic one (managers of people who now manage AI armies).

### Structure
- **Headers state the point, not tease it.** Pyramid Principle: every header should tell the reader what the section argues. No "The Punchline" — lead with the answer, then support it.
- **Pyramid Principle is the editorial standard.** Governing thought first. Support below. Chronological process walkthroughs need to be restructured around the argument, not the timeline. But context-setting openers linking to previous articles are still needed for standalone readability.
- **Series articles open with linked context.** When an article builds on previous ones, the opener should name and link the predecessors, summarize what they established, and state what this article adds.

### Precision and framing rules (from editing cycles)
- **Be precise about who does what.** The system handles rote code management (testing, merging, documentation, convention enforcement). Wes provides domain expertise and strategic direction. Misattributing agency — saying the system implements domain knowledge when Wes does — undermines the thesis. Get the mechanics right.
- **Frame bidirectional systems as bidirectional.** The proposal system is both bottom-up (agents surfacing issues) and top-down (Wes injecting strategic direction). Any system that works both ways must be presented as such. Missing one direction makes the system sound passive.
- **Address reader anxieties head-on.** Engineers have strong priors (merge conflicts are hard, AI code is unreliable, etc.). Glossing over these loses credibility with the exact audience reading this. Name the concern, then explain why it's handled.
- **Honest about imperfection.** Never present the system as working perfectly. The Claude Code "never ever do this" aside, the "will never achieve flawless execution" framing — these build trust. Overselling undermines everything.
- **Examples must be framed as examples.** "For example" or "in one case" matters. Without explicit framing, a specific instance reads as the only instance.
- **Tie back to management principles.** When a system behavior maps to a core principle from the About page (e.g., "trust scales with demonstrated judgment"), make the connection explicitly. Link to weswest.ai when possible. Builds a coherent body of work across articles.
- **Asides for Management 101 connections.** When the article describes a system behavior that maps to basic management (manage by objective, don't micromanage), note the connection — but as an aside or footnote, not as the main text. The reader should see the parallel without being lectured.
