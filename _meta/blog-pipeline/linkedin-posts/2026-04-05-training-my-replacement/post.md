Saw one of those "with AI I feel like I'm training my replacement" posts on Reddit. No duh.

I've been saying for the last year that the mental model for AI-assisted development is a forced promotion from individual contributor to team manager. The problem is that a huge fraction of technical talent explicitly never wanted to be managers — they chose an IC track because they wanted to go deep on craft. AI tools require exactly the skills they structured their careers to avoid.

It's hard. And if you didn't choose it, it sucks: the agency to build a career on one's own terms is removed. The reality, though, of being a good manager is that "training my replacement" is a core KPI: you clarify your objectives, document your judgment, and enable others to execute on your behalf. You work yourself out of a job, and the minute you succeed, you get handed a bigger task and the process starts all over again.

This is going to be a rough transition for the next few years.
