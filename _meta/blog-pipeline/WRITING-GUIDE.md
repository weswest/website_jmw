# Personal Blog Writing Guide

This document defines the strategic objectives, voice, and guardrails for personal blog content.

---

## Strategic Objectives

### What are we trying to prove?

1. **Skilled people leader** — I've been around the block. Not perfect, but I've seen things and learned from them. I make mistakes and own them. I've managed through hard situations.

2. **C-Suite caliber** — Extensive executive experience. Comfortable in boardrooms. Understand how senior leaders think and what they need.

3. **Macro-to-micro range** — Can evaluate problems at the strategic level, understand macro context and objectives, AND get deeply into the weeds of actual delivery. This is rare. Most people are one or the other.

4. **Translator** — Can do sophisticated analytical work AND make it accessible to senior, non-technical leaders. Feynman-style: if I can't explain it simply, I don't understand it well enough.

5. **Very smart** — Especially relevant for analytics roles. Even people-focused pieces should demonstrate intellectual depth, pattern recognition, ability to synthesize complex situations into insight.

6. **Real** — As far from AI slop as possible. Messy where life is messy. Honest about failures. No neat bows.

7. **Creative** — Especially in deployment of AI and modeling technologies. Novel approaches. Willingness to question conventional wisdom.

8. **Self-aware and still growing** — I have blind spots. Leaving consulting forced me to get better at helping people untangle their thoughts. I'm not the finished product.

### Open agenda (what readers should take away)
- This person has hard-won management wisdom worth learning from
- This person thinks clearly about complex organizational problems
- This person would be interesting to work with/for

### Hidden agenda (what we're actually doing)
- Positioning for C-suite analytics roles
- Building credibility for eventual book
- Creating content that demonstrates leadership capability to potential employers
- Establishing personal brand distinct from current employer

## Target Audience

- Analytics leaders and aspiring analytics leaders
- Banking/financial services executives
- Potential employers, boards, recruiters
- People who might buy a management book someday

## Voice & Tone

### Core characteristics
- **Confident but not arrogant** — Strong assertions, but willing to say "I got this wrong"
- **Slightly controversial** — Willing to challenge conventional wisdom, call out uncomfortable truths
- **Focused** — Every paragraph earns its place. No filler.
- **Grounded in specifics** — Real examples, real numbers, real situations (anonymized)
- **Intellectually sophisticated without being academic** — Use precise words when they're the right words, but don't show off

### What to do
- Lead with confident assertions
- Use specific anecdotes with concrete details
- Include numbers where they strengthen the point (10 weeks → 1.5 weeks)
- Ask rhetorical questions that land
- Admit mistakes and uncertainty where real
- Use "I" naturally
- Deploy sophisticated vocabulary casually when it fits
- End with honest uncertainty rather than false resolution

### What to avoid
- Tidy lessons-learned bullets
- "Here's my 5-step framework"
- Overly positive spin on failures
- Generic advice that could apply to anyone
- Excessive metaphors (pick one, use it sparingly)
- Filler paragraphs that don't advance the argument
- AI tells: "Here's the thing:", "Let's dive in", "In conclusion"

---

## Structure Guidelines

### Length
- Default target: **600-800 words**
- Longer only if the material demands it
- If it can be said in fewer words, use fewer words

### Hooks
- Should provoke or intrigue
- Avoid clickbait
- Best hooks: contrarian assertions, surprising specifics, honest confessions
- Test: Would I click on this if someone else wrote it?

### Endings
- Don't tie it up in a bow
- Honest uncertainty > false resolution
- "I don't have a clean framework" is fine
- Leave the reader thinking, not satisfied

## Pre-Flight Checklist

Before publishing, ask:

**Strategic**
- [ ] Does this demonstrate people leadership experience?
- [ ] Does this demonstrate intellectual depth?
- [ ] Does this feel real and honest?
- [ ] Would a potential employer be impressed by this?

**Voice**
- [ ] Is there a confident assertion in the first two paragraphs?
- [ ] Are there specific examples with concrete details?
- [ ] Does it sound like me, not a LinkedIn thought leader?
- [ ] Have I admitted uncertainty or failure somewhere?

**Craft**
- [ ] Can any paragraph be cut without losing the piece?
- [ ] Is the hook actually good?
- [ ] Does the ending avoid false tidiness?
- [ ] Is it under 800 words? If not, why?

## LinkedIn Adaptation

When posting to LinkedIn:
- Pull the hook into the first line
- Front-load the contrarian take
- Keep the full piece on the personal site, excerpt for LinkedIn
- End with something that invites discussion, not a CTA

## Topics That Serve the Strategy

**High value** (demonstrates leadership + smarts):
- Team transformation stories
- Managing through ambiguity
- Navigating organizational politics
- Hiring/firing decisions and regrets
- Technical leadership without technical expertise

**Medium value** (demonstrates smarts, less leadership):
- AI/ML implementation lessons
- Modeling approaches and pitfalls
- Data strategy

**Lower value** (may not serve positioning):
- Pure technical tutorials
- Industry news commentary
- Generic career advice
