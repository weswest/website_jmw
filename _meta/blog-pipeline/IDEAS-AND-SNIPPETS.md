## Concepts Worth Developing

**The third axis of performance evaluation:**
We evaluate people on capability and motivation. But there's a third axis: does this person instinctively question whether the work should exist in its current form? Some people execute tasks forever. Others are allergic to unnecessary work—they'd invest a month to avoid doing something the hard way indefinitely.

**"Lazy in the programmer way":**
Not lazy as in avoidant—lazy as in hating repetitive work so much you'll invest heavily to eliminate it. Jianglan didn't care about the job more than Sarah. She just couldn't tolerate inefficiency.

**The evaluation horizon problem:**
She was optimizing within the existing approach (doing 10 weeks efficiently). The right move was stepping back to ask whether the 10-week process should exist at all. That requires a 2-month investment to save 8 weeks per quarter. Obvious math in retrospect—but nobody was doing the math.

**Managing work you don't know how to do:**
First time I had to trust employees to do work I didn't have hands-on experience doing myself. Every previous job, I'd come up through the ranks doing exactly what my team did. This changes everything about how you evaluate and develop people.

**"Fine" as a danger signal:**
The struggling employees get managed out. The system handles them. The fine employees accumulate dysfunction for years because nobody has a specific complaint.

## Potential Article Ideas

**The Vortex Anti-Pattern:**
A manager burned by past mistakes institutes collective review paranoia—entire team spends hours reviewing numbers line by line. Fear-based quality control that destroys morale and productivity. How to break the pattern.

**From Box-Checking to Strategic Powerhouse:**
Full transformation story of the treasury analytics team. Purpose shift as the core lever. How changing what the team was *for* changed everything else. (Multiple posts possible from Q2 material)

**Developing Managers to Diagnose Problems:**
Skip-level management. The modeling manager who learned to interrogate complexity through this exercise and others—went on to have an amazing career as a problem solver. How do you teach this skill?

**The Mini-Forecasting Engine:**
Understanding the full chain of work so deeply you can instantly estimate how changes ripple through. The value of being the person who can field "what if" questions in real-time. How to develop this.

**Managing Through What You Don't Understand:**
When you can't do the work yourself, what signals do you watch for? How do you evaluate people? How do you know when to dive in vs. trust?

**Navigating Market Discontinuities (from Q3):**
LIBOR→SOFR transition, negative rates scenario. When 70% is insight and 30% is data. CEO's "two simulations" approach—business as usual vs. catastrophizing. The value of planned catastrophizing.

**The Model Factory Story (from Q1):**
Scaling CCAR modeling with junior staff. Three-legged stool: statistically valid, accurate, makes business sense. Homegrown MLOps before MLOps existed. Assembly-line approach to regulatory modeling.

**Ablation Is the Name of the Game (STANDALONE):**
In any analytic endeavor you can only change one variable at a time. Ablation studies—systematically removing components to understand their contribution—are how you actually understand complex systems. Most people change three things at once and can't explain what worked. This applies to modeling, process improvement, organizational change. Strong standalone piece.

**Locally Perfect, Collectively Destructive (series with Sausage):**
NII/EVE inconsistency story. Each component decision was locally rational, but the system was incoherent. Build a table, make complexity visible, force documentation of what's actually happening. The unlock is usually embarrassingly simple. Sylvia's development arc.

**System-Level Thinking vs. Component Thinking:**
The component isn't the unit of analysis. The system is. Most people optimize their piece. The valuable ones understand how their piece interacts with everything else and flag when the collective doesn't make sense.

## Voice/Style Notes Captured

- Lead with confident assertions, not throat-clearing
- Use specific numbers (10 weeks → 1.5 weeks, 85% reduction)
- Admit failures directly: "That's on me," "I should have caught this"
- Rhetorical questions that land, sparingly
- "That's bitten me" as a pivot from principle to story
- No tidy lessons-learned bullets at the end
- Sophisticated vocabulary deployed casually, not showily
- Default 600-800 words; shorter is usually better
