## Four Pillars

**1. See all of the options.**
The first step isn't building — it's mapping the full solution space so you know what you're choosing between. Business objectives are achieved through tangible work products, and each one carries its own cost/benefit profile.

**2. Optimize the cost/benefit tradeoff.**
Accuracy, interpretability, speed to production, regulatory burden — every business decision is a multi-dimensional tradeoff. "Most accurate" or "highest ROI" is rarely the best answer. Twenty years across analytics, strategy, and operations have revealed which tradeoffs in which contexts actually matter.

**3. Execute efficiently.**
Whether it's a model build, a platform migration, or a product rollout — the skill is the same: decompose, sequence, find the bottleneck, solve it. The right sequencing means early deliverables generate value while the rest are still being built.

**4. Make it land.**
Analytics is only as valuable as the decision it changes. Board and C-suite audiences don't need to understand the methodology — they need to understand the implications, the tradeoffs, and recommendations.

## Five Principles

**1. Lead from the front.**
I personally understand the complexities, constraints, and opportunities of the systems I lead. I don't manage from a remove.

**2. Respect the decisions that got us here …**
Established approaches exist because someone made a reasonable tradeoff with the information they had. Understanding *why* things are the way they are is a prerequisite to changing them.

**3. … Then change them.**
"We've always done it this way" is not a reason to keep doing it. Objectives evolve. Constraints shift. The work is knowing when the old tradeoff no longer holds.

**4. Trust in my team scales with demonstrated judgment.**
I give people as much autonomy as they've earned. The management job is calibrating how much independence each person can handle — and then staying out of the way.

**5. Build the 30-page appendix so the 3-page executive summary is bulletproof.**
I want to know every detail that could matter — not to present all of it, but so that when someone challenges a conclusion, I'm not guessing.

## Voice, Style, and Writing Guide

This section captures the nuances of how Wes wants to present himself. These are the details that make the difference between copy that sounds right and copy that needs four rounds of revision.

### Tone and posture
- **"No duh" framing.** Pillars and principles should read as obvious truths about how good work gets done — not as personal accomplishments or marketing claims. The reader should think "of course, that's how it should work" and then realize most people don't actually operate this way. The career arc and articles provide the proof; the philosophy sections don't need to justify themselves.
- **Professional, direct.** No buddy energy. No forced warmth. No "I'm passionate about..." language. Think: the way a confident executive talks in a first meeting with a peer. Declarative, not performative.
- **The site should feel like it was made by someone who knows what they're doing, not by someone trying to impress you.** This is the meta-principle for all design and copy decisions.
- **Never self-aggrandizing.** Wes is abnormally smart and his track record is exceptional, but the site should never say so. The evidence makes it obvious. Let the reader draw the conclusion.

### Language preferences
- **Verb nouns. Action-oriented language.** Wes explicitly prefers verbing nouns because they're more active. "Make it land" was chosen over alternatives partly for this reason. Favor constructions that do things over constructions that describe things.
- **Problem-framing over experience-framing.** When choosing between "I have 20 years of experience doing X" and "The first step isn't building — it's mapping the full solution space," choose the second. Lead with the problem or the principle; let the experience appear naturally as support. Pillar 1 was chosen specifically because "it talks about the problem, not my experience."
- **Outcome-first.** Don't describe what was done; describe what changed. Not "I built a model factory" but "this technique now forecasts >10% of US retail bank deposits."
- **Cut ruthlessly.** When something is too long, cut it. Wes will push back on verbose copy. If a pillar description is three sentences and it works at one, use one. Short + punchy beats thorough + accurate.
- **Avoid consulting jargon and vague "innovation" phrasing.** No "leveraging synergies," no "driving transformation," no "thought leader." Plain language that a CEO would actually use in conversation.
- **Em dashes for asides, not parentheses.** This matches Wes's natural writing style and the pillar/principle copy already established.

### Specific rhetorical devices Wes likes
- **Paired/linked assertions.** Principles #2 and #3 use ellipses ("Respect the decisions that got us here …" / "… Then change them.") to read as one thought with a pivot. This intentional linking structure was Wes's idea. When two ideas form a natural pair, this device works.
- **The "intellectual gun to a knife fight" attitude.** Wes wants to be so over-prepared that any challenge can be met with overwhelming detail — but the surface presentation is restrained. Principle #5 captures this. The underlying attitude should inform all copy: confidence backed by depth, never bluster.
- **The complexity tax as signature metaphor.** Building something sophisticated you'll never deploy, specifically so you can have the right conversation about why the simpler thing is the right answer. This is Wes's best story and his value proposition in miniature. Reference it, link to it, use it as the anchoring example whenever the "depth + communication" thesis needs proof.
- **Concrete specifics over abstract claims.** "20-minute conversation with the CEO, CFO, head of marketing, and head of retail" lands harder than "executive stakeholder alignment." Name the rooms, name the roles, name the numbers. $200B balance sheet. 40M households. >10% of US deposits. eNPS from -45 to +92.

### What NOT to do
- **Never mention the patent.** Not obliquely, not as "IP development," not as "novel proprietary architecture." The work itself (AI agents, human-in-the-loop workflows, structured domain reasoning, deterministic AI approaches) is fair game. The fact that a patent exists is not.
- **Never position as job-seeking.** No "open to opportunities," no "hire me" CTAs, no "available for" language. The site shows what Wes does and how he thinks. The reader decides whether to reach out.
- **Never use "Stay Positive" or personal creative writing.** That world doesn't exist on this site.
- **No blog-style casualness.** Even recipes (if they appear) should live in "Writing" and be treated as real content, not filler.
- **Don't over-explain the principles.** They are assertions. The descriptions are one follow-up sentence, not a paragraph of justification. If a principle needs a paragraph to land, the principle statement is wrong.

### Editorial mode: "choice points"
When presenting copy alternatives, show the variants first, then isolate the 2-4 specific decision axes (e.g., "I" vs. gerund, "hard" vs. "complex") with a one-line rationale for each option. This lets Wes make precise editorial decisions on the individual dimensions instead of choosing between whole blocks where the differences are buried. Matches his decomposition mindset.

### How Wes edits
Understanding Wes's editing style will save iterations:
- He reacts to drafts. Give him something concrete to push back on, not options described abstractly.
- He'll tell you the *axes are wrong* before he'll wordsmith. If the framing is off, no amount of polishing fixes it. Get the framing right first.
- Specific word choices matter. "Early deliverables" not "first deliverables." "Have revealed" not "means I know." He notices and corrects.
- He'll expand scope when the structure is right (3 pillars became 4 when the decomposition clicked).
- He'll cut aggressively when something is too long and tell you when something is too short.
- He gives raw, unpolished substance and expects it to be cleaned up while keeping the edge. "I don't know how to frame that correctly" means "polish this but don't lose what I'm saying."
